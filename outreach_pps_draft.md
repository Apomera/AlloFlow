My name is Dr. Aaron Pomeranz — I'm a school psychologist and the creator of AlloFlow, an open-source AI differentiation platform designed specifically for diverse learners, including students with IEPs, 504 plans, and complex communication needs.

I'm reaching out because I've been following Portland Public Schools' work on AI integration, including the Lumi Story AI pilot. I understand the district is navigating the intersection of innovation, teacher voice, and student privacy — challenges I've spent the last six months building solutions for.

**I'd love to show you what we've built — and why it might be worth a conversation.**

### What AlloFlow Offers

AlloFlow is a single platform with **80+ instructional tools** — not a single-purpose app. Among them is **StoryForge**, a scaffolded creative writing studio that provides the same core functionality as Lumi Story AI:

- AI-generated story scaffolds, illustrations (Imagen 4.0), and narration (8 TTS voices)
- Comic panel and prose layouts with student-directed image refinement
- AI grading with Glow/Grow feedback, custom rubric support, and vocabulary scoring
- 18+ language support, speech-to-text dictation, and WCAG accessibility
- Student achievement system, writing analytics, and teacher progress monitoring

But StoryForge is just one of 80+ tools. AlloFlow also includes a full STEM lab (41 tools), SEL hub, AAC/symbol communication, clinical reporting suite (FBA/BIP, psychoeducational report writer), RTI dashboard, adaptive games, and more.

### Why This Might Matter for PPS

I built AlloFlow because I work with the students who fall through the cracks of one-size-fits-all edtech. Three things differentiate it from commercial alternatives:

1. **Cost: $0.** AlloFlow is open-source (GNU AGPL v3). No per-student licensing, no contracts. The $148K allocated to Lumi for 9 schools could serve the entire district — or be redirected to direct student services.

2. **Privacy by architecture.** AlloFlow processes AI on the client side. Zero student PII is stored in the cloud. When running inside Google Canvas, all sharing and cloud features are automatically disabled (FERPA double-gated). We don't collect data to train models. We don't need data agreements because we don't collect data.

3. **Teacher empowerment, not replacement.** Every AI feature in AlloFlow is teacher-controlled. Teachers set the rubrics, choose the vocabulary, configure the scaffolds, and review all AI outputs before students see them. AI is positioned as a teaching aide — not a substitute for human instruction.

I'm not selling anything. I'd simply like to offer a 30-minute demo to your team and whoever oversees instructional technology. If it's not the right fit, no hard feelings. But if PPS is exploring AI tools for literacy and differentiation, I think you'll find this worth seeing.

I'm happy to work within whatever evaluation framework your office uses. AlloFlow can run on Firebase Hosting, air-gapped Docker, or directly in Google Gemini Canvas.

Would you or a colleague be open to a brief conversation?

## Email 3: To Chris Schweizer / PAT Leadership (Teacher Union Outreach)

**Subject:** An AI Tool Built by an Educator, for Educators — Not to Replace Them

---

Dear Mr. Schweizer (and PAT colleagues),

My name is Dr. Aaron Pomeranz. I'm a school psychologist who has spent the last six months building an open-source educational platform called AlloFlow. I'm reaching out because I deeply respect the concerns PAT has raised about AI in Portland Public Schools — and I think there's a version of this technology that addresses them.

I share your core belief: **students learn best from humans.** That's why I built AlloFlow differently.

### AI as a Teaching Aide, Not a Teacher

Every AI feature in AlloFlow is controlled by the teacher. The AI doesn't talk to students directly. It doesn't run lessons. It doesn't make decisions. Instead, it does the things that drain teachers' time — generating differentiated scaffolds at 5 reading levels, creating visual supports for non-verbal students, producing IEP-aligned materials — so teachers can spend more time actually teaching.

Here's a concrete example: AlloFlow's creative writing tool (StoryForge) doesn't write stories for students. It generates scaffold frames that the teacher reviews and approves. Students write their own paragraphs. The AI illustrates what the student wrote (not what the AI wrote). The teacher sees every piece of student work and grades it using their own rubric — or the AI offers suggestions that the teacher can accept, modify, or reject.

### What Makes This Different from Lumi

- **It's free and open-source.** No corporate contracts. No per-student fees. No VC-backed company profiting from student data.
- **It doesn't collect student data.** Everything runs in the browser. Nothing goes to the cloud unless a teacher explicitly pushes it — and even that's blocked in Canvas environments.
- **It was built by a school psychologist.** I work with students on IEPs and 504 plans. I built this because the tools that exist don't serve the students who need them most.
- **It's not just a writing tool.** AlloFlow has 80+ tools spanning STEM, SEL, AAC, clinical reporting, and adaptive games. It's a platform, not a product.

### Why I'm Writing to You

I'm not asking you to endorse AI in schools. I'm asking whether there's a version of AI integration that PAT could support — one where teachers are in control, student privacy is protected by design, and the technology genuinely reduces workload rather than replacing human judgment.

If you'd be willing to see a demo, I'd be honored. And if after seeing it you still have concerns, I want to hear them — because the educators who push back on edtech are usually the ones who understand what actually works in classrooms.

With respect,

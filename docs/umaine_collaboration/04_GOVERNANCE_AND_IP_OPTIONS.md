# Governance and intellectual-property options

**Status:** Negotiation framework only. This is not legal advice, an offer, an assignment, or a license. The parties should use independent counsel and UMaine's authorized offices.

## Recommendation

Begin with **Model A: collaboration plus durable license and joint steering**. Consider assignment only after a successful pilot, mutual diligence, and an institutional sustainability decision.

Do not transfer the trademark, domains, or copyright merely to secure a meeting or make a grant proposal. An unconditional gift would not require UMS to maintain, distribute, employ anyone, or continue AlloFlow.

## Non-negotiable public-interest principles

1. AlloFlow's source remains available under AGPL-3.0-or-later or a successor strong-copyleft license approved through the governance process.
2. Existing public license grants are not revoked.
3. No contributor or institution may convert the community edition into closed source.
4. Research findings, including negative findings, remain publishable under normal academic rules.
5. Governance includes educators, disability/accessibility representation, and community maintainers—not only institutional administrators.
6. Trademark rules protect users from misleading or unsafe distributions but do not suppress good-faith forks.
7. If the institutional steward stops maintaining the project, stewardship can move rather than strand the project.

## Assets that must be treated separately

“AlloFlow” is not one transferable object. Diligence must separately identify:

- copyright in existing code and documentation;
- third-party code, models, fonts, media, datasets, and licenses;
- the AlloFlow name, logo, goodwill, and any trademark applications or registrations;
- domains, social accounts, package names, signing keys, and app-store identities;
- GitHub and Codeberg organizations and repositories;
- deployment accounts and cloud projects;
- contributor permissions and provenance;
- study data and future institutional works; and
- confidential credentials, which should never be transferred as IP.

The current repository is AGPL-licensed, but a copyright/provenance inventory is still required before anyone promises clean assignment of the entire codebase.

## Model A — Collaboration MOU + perpetual institutional license

**Best first step.** Aaron retains pre-existing copyright and trademark. UMS receives a perpetual, worldwide, royalty-free license to use, reproduce, modify, distribute, host, preserve, and continue the AGPL project for education, research, and public service. A joint steering group governs the official collaboration branch and research roadmap.

Advantages:

- enough rights for UMS to conduct research and continue the work;
- no premature transfer;
- simpler exit if the collaboration is not a fit;
- preserves leverage to negotiate actual maintenance commitments later.

Important terms:

- pre-existing IP schedule;
- license scope and AGPL continuity;
- official-project/trademark use;
- research independence and publication;
- security and incident roles;
- contribution ownership for grant-funded work;
- decision rights, deadlock, term, and exit;
- no promise of employment or funding absent a separate written agreement.

## Model B — Joint public-interest stewardship

Create a formal project council under a UMS center, fiscal sponsor, or independent nonprofit/foundation. Copyright may remain distributed among contributors while the steward controls official releases and holds the trademark under a mission lock.

This is attractive if AlloFlow becomes a multi-institution community. It requires more administration and should follow evidence of adoption and maintainer participation.

## Model C — Assignment to UMS with safeguards

Aaron assigns specified pre-existing copyright and/or the trademark to UMS. This may simplify institutional control, but it should occur only if UMS accepts written stewardship obligations.

Candidate protections:

- perpetual strong-copyleft public release commitment;
- no exclusive relicensing that would impair the public edition;
- defined minimum maintenance and preservation duties, subject to funding realities;
- continued public repository and issue tracker;
- community governance and transparent roadmap;
- Aaron's attribution and a defined governance role that is independent of employment status;
- a reversion or transfer-to-successor mechanism after a defined period of abandonment, subject to sponsor and third-party obligations;
- transition access to domains, release keys, documentation, and build infrastructure;
- treatment of future grant-funded work under UMS policy;
- an express statement that IP transfer does not guarantee employment.

UMS's current IP policy covers software, copyrightable works, trademarks, sponsored work, work-for-hire, and significant use of university resources. It generally leaves copyright with creators subject to important exceptions, while institutional and assigned programming work may belong to UMS. The authorized UMS offices—not an individual faculty member—must approve institutional IP terms. See [UMS Board Policy 209](https://www.maine.edu/board-of-trustees/policy-manual/section-209/) and the [full UMS IP policy](https://www.maine.edu/board-of-trustees/wp-content/uploads/sites/12/2019/12/intprop.pdf).

## Proposed five-seat steering council for Model A

1. UMaine research lead or designee.
2. AlloFlow technical steward (initially Aaron).
3. Practicing educator selected by the council.
4. Accessibility/disability-community representative, compensated where possible.
5. Independent open-source or public-interest technology representative.

Suggested decision rules:

- ordinary roadmap decisions: simple majority;
- license, trademark assignment, privacy boundary, or project dissolution: four of five;
- research methods, data, and publication: controlled by the PI/institutional research process, not the product council;
- security response: designated maintainers may act immediately, followed by council review;
- members disclose conflicts and recuse when deciding their own compensation or employment.

## Nonbinding term-sheet outline

| Topic | Starting position |
|---|---|
| Pre-existing code | Aaron retains ownership during pilot; UMS receives broad perpetual AGPL-compatible rights |
| Grant-funded new code | Determine under sponsor terms and UMS policy before proposal submission; require public AGPL release where sponsor permits |
| Trademark | Aaron retains initially; UMS receives limited official-collaboration use under quality rules |
| Official repository | Public; protected branch and documented release process |
| Research control | UMaine PI controls methods, data analysis, and publication |
| Employment | Separate grant budget and UMS appointment; no link making scientific conclusions a condition of employment |
| Governance | Five-seat council with educator and disability representation |
| Exit | Continued public license plus orderly transfer of official-project assets; assignment model includes abandonment/reversion mechanism |
| Revenue | No requirement to monetize; grants, gifts, and cost-recovery services permitted if consistent with sponsor and UMS rules |

## Questions for UMS counsel/IP and ORA

- Which UMS entity would sign: the Board of Trustees, UMaine, a center, or another unit?
- Can UMS accept stewardship of an existing AGPL project and trademark under current policy?
- Would UMS prefer assignment, a broad license, or a sponsored-project background-IP schedule?
- How would UMS treat pre-existing code versus grant-funded modifications?
- Can the agreement include a successor-steward or reversion mechanism if maintenance ends?
- Which office can approve trademark quality-control terms?
- What contributor agreement or developer certificate of origin would UMS require?
- What conflict disclosures are required if the creator is employed on the project?

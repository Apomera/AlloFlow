### 1. Competence and Insanity Address Different Times and Legal Questions

Placement: ch-6, section 2 — Forensic Assessment: Competency vs. Insanity

Expanded the full text alternative and made the Dusky abilities, retrospective insanity inquiry, evaluator role, and jurisdiction-specific legal decision explicit.

- [Dusky v. United States, 362 U.S. 402 (1960)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/362/402/) — Supreme Court of the United States; case text hosted by Justia. This is the controlling U.S. Supreme Court decision stating the constitutional minimum for present competence: sufficient ability to consult with counsel with rational understanding and a rational as well as factual understanding of the proceedings.
- [AAPL Practice Guideline for the Forensic Psychiatric Evaluation of Competence to Stand Trial](https://www.aapl.org/docs/pdf/Competence%20to%20Stand%20Trial.pdf) — American Academy of Psychiatry and the Law; Journal of the American Academy of Psychiatry and the Law. This specialty-society practice guideline synthesizes governing law and accepted forensic assessment practice, emphasizes functional and jurisdiction-specific analysis, and distinguishes an evaluator’s evidence and opinion from the court’s legal determination.
- [AAPL Practice Guideline for Forensic Psychiatric Evaluation of Defendants Raising the Insanity Defense](https://www.aapl.org/docs/pdf/Insanity%20Defense%20Guidelines.pdf) — American Academy of Psychiatry and the Law; Journal of the American Academy of Psychiatry and the Law. This peer-reviewed specialty guideline addresses retrospective evaluation of mental state at the time of an alleged offense and repeatedly anchors conclusions to the particular jurisdiction’s legal test rather than treating insanity as a diagnosis or universal rule.
- [18 U.S. Code § 17 — Insanity defense](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/17) — Legal Information Institute, Cornell Law School; source text from the United States Code. Cornell Law School’s Legal Information Institute reproduces the federal statute and links its source and amendments. It demonstrates that insanity criteria and burdens are legal rules and that the federal formulation is not a universal clinical definition.

Review note: Diagram wording, labels, and placement were corrected for accuracy, nuance, source alignment, and learner interpretation. Expanded the full text alternative and made the Dusky abilities, retrospective insanity inquiry, evaluator role, and jurisdiction-specific legal decision explicit. Independent qualified expert validation remains pending.

- [The Integrated Developmental Model of Supervision](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=ED302789) — ERIC, Institute of Education Sciences, U.S. Department of Education. This federal education-research record indexes Cal Stoltenberg’s original IDM presentation and abstract. It identifies motivation, self-and-other awareness, autonomy, multiple professional domains, three developmental levels, and a fourth integrated level.
- [The integrative developmental model of supervision](https://doi.org/10.1002/9781118846360.ch28) — The Wiley International Handbook of Clinical Supervision, John Wiley & Sons. This authored handbook chapter is a later scholarly account of IDM by Stoltenberg and colleagues. It reviews Levels 1–3 and 3i, the model’s research base, and professional development across domains rather than treating a level as a global competence verdict.
- [Development of the Supervisee Levels Questionnaire—Revised](https://doi.org/10.1037/0735-7028.23.6.504) — Professional Psychology: Research and Practice, American Psychological Association. This peer-reviewed APA journal article by McNeill and colleagues documents measurement of IDM’s motivation, autonomy, and self/other awareness structures. It provides traceable empirical context for the model while not converting its profiles into licensure decisions.

Review note: Diagram wording, labels, and placement were corrected for accuracy, nuance, source alignment, and learner interpretation.
